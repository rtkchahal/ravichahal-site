"Not your keys, not your coins" has been the Bitcoin community's rallying cry since Mt. Gox. Sixteen years later, after FTX, after Celsius, after a dozen smaller collapses, it's still not being heeded by the majority of holders. Let's fix that.

Self-custody is not complicated. It requires discipline, not technical genius. Here is the actual threat model and how to address it.

## The Threats

**Exchange insolvency.** The exchange holds your Bitcoin in a pool. If they go insolvent, misappropriate funds, or get hacked, your Bitcoin is a creditor claim — not a property right. This is the most common risk and the one exchange users consistently underestimate.

**Phishing and account takeover.** Your email gets compromised. The attacker resets your exchange password. Your Bitcoin is gone in under three minutes. No recourse.

**Regulatory seizure.** In a jurisdictional crackdown, exchanges are obligated to comply with government orders to freeze and seize assets. Self-custodied Bitcoin requires a court order against *you specifically*.

## The Stack

For most people, the right self-custody stack is:

1. **Hardware wallet** (Coldcard Mk4 or Foundation Passport) for long-term cold storage
2. **Mobile wallet** (Phoenix or Mutiny) for small amounts on Lightning
3. **Seed phrase backup** on steel, not paper, stored separately from the device

The hardware wallet holds the bulk of your stack. The mobile wallet is your spending wallet. Never more than you'd carry as cash.

## The Ceremony

Setting up cold storage properly means: generating keys on an air-gapped device, verifying the seed phrase by restoring it before funding, and testing a small withdrawal before committing serious funds.

It takes two hours to do right. The cost of doing it wrong is everything. Self-custody is not paranoia — it's the entire point of Bitcoin.
